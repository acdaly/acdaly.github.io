OVERVIEW


Our team was challenged to create a responsive website design for the food trucks in the Carnegie Mellon area. To better understand the needs of food truck consumers, we conducted interviews and observations, synthesized our findings, and created personas, scenarios, and storyboards over a two week period.

This research was used to determine how a website could improve consumer experiences. We created CMU To Go, a responsive website design that will inform food truck consumers of whether the truck is open, its typical hours, and give consumers the ability to order their food for pickup.




RESEARCH PHASE



How can we improve the consumer experience at the local food trucks and cars?


We first set out to identify what motivated consumers to eat at the local food trucks and cars, and what issues there are in their current experience. We decided to narrow our scope to the food trucks and cars along Carnegie Mellon campus, since these dining options are frequented by many students, but much of the information about them is hard to find online.



The

"food trucks"
refer to the trucks that have been parked by the campus soccer field for several years.



The

"food cars"
refer to cars run by local Chinese restaurants that sell lunch sets from their trunks. Their location sometimes changes per semester, and the cars don't have any signage. The menu, location, and hours are advertised through a Chinese WeChat group.



Observations & Interviews


We conducted

observations
on the food cars and trucks and 6
interviews
with a food truck owner and food truck/car customers. It was important for us to hear different perspectives, so we were sure to interview different types of customers. We interviewed both undergraduate and graduate students, domestic and international students, and a staff member.

Our goal was to find out what brought customers to the food trucks/cars, reasons why they chose not to eat there certain days, when they expected the trucks/cars to be open, and the pain points in their experience. For the food truck owner, we wanted to find out the actual open hours, how weather affected whether they opened, and other factors that affect their sales.



Insight Synthesis





We synthesized our research and were able to identify insights on 3 main consumer pain points.


BAD WEATHER


Customers said they were much less likely to go to these vendors when the weather was bad. Since there's nearby indoor seating and the walk to these vendors is comparable to other campus dining options, the main issue with bad weather wass the time spent waiting in line and for food.

Most of the customer's time is spent waiting for food.

LACK OF CASH


Customers didn't go to the food trucks and cars when they don't have cash on them, but these vendors all accept alternate pay options (venmo, credit card, or online payment). These options are not well advertised, so customers are not aware they can pay without cash.

People don't know they can pay with venmo/credit.

LACK OF INFORMATION


There's little information online regarding these food trucks and cars, resulting in people not knowing where these vehicles are or when they're open.

There's no main hub of information for these mobile vendors.



Personas


Based off of our research, created 3 user personas.



Darren Carpenter

Graduate Student

“I'm excited to continue my education, but the industry paycheck would've been nice.”

Darren is a graduate student at Carnegie Mellon University, getting his PhD in neuroscience. He came to Carnegie Mellon in 2015, after completing his undergrad at NYU. As he continues to further his education, he lives frugally so as not to break his student budget.

In the city, Darren grew accustomed to a having a variety of reasonably priced vegetarian options within walking distance, and had a hard time adjusting to the lack of options in Pittsburgh and particularly around campus. His daily struggle is finding a good, quick meal at a reasonable price.

In the winter months, Darren finds that even snow will not deter him from visiting the trucks, especially as they are so close to his housing. He admits, however, that the dreary Pittsburgh rain will cause him to seek indoor options instead.







Joy Guo

Undergraduate Student

“Just trying to get by, one homework at a time.”

Joy is a sophomore at Carnegie Mellon University, majoring in Electrical and Computer Engineering. Outside of schoolwork, she keeps herself busy with school clubs like Buggy, and is in a sorority.

Joy grew up in the Bay Area. Her parents are first generation immigrants from China, so she grew up eating Chinese food at home. She is often going from activity to activity, so she rarely has time to cook. She is disappointed by the choices of Chinese food in Pittsburgh, but especially of campus food, and enjoys authentic Chinese food from the food cars once a week.

Most of her meals she picks up on the go. Joy doesn't speak fluent Chinese and doesn't use Wechat, so she sometimes can't find the food cars and doesn't know what will be on the menu on a given day.







Paula Peterson

Administrative Coordinator

“I love seeing Carnegie Mellon grow while still holding true to its values and traditions.”

Paula Peterson is an Administrative Coordinator at Carnegie Mellon University and is 35 years-old. She moved from Philadelphia to begin her undergraduate career at Carnegie Mellon in 2002, and transitioned to working at the university after graduation.

Though Paula has left the days of eating with a student meal plan behind, she does still get food at the food trucks on Margaret Morrison Street. She doesn't visit the trucks too often anymore, but she gets food from the trucks on the way to work once or twice a month when she's coming in late.She's less inclined to visit the trucks during the colder months, and especially when the weather is particularly bad.

Paula still visits the same food truck she used to when she was an undergraduate. She doesn't mind that otherwise not much has changed, but she does wish that she knew which cars would be open when she walks over.




Scenarios & Storyboards



We created storyboards and scenarios to invision how our website might function.



After coming up with and discussing these storyboards and scenarios, we decided our website should be a hub of information for the CMU food trucks and cars, including their hours, menu, dietary offerrings, and payment options, and a website where users could order food in advance. We had thought about having a peer-to-peer delivery option, but decided that was more of a mobile app feature and that being able to order for pickup would alleviate the main issue of the food truck/car experience when the weather is bad.


PROTOTYPING PHASE



Competitive Analysis




We looked at Yelp, and cmuEats, Grubhub, and Postmates to understand current design conventions and how our website stands out from existing sites.

cmuEats is a Carnegie Mellon specific website that is popular for many students. It displays the on campus dining options and whether or not they are open. Its popularity shows the need for a similar hub of information for the campus food trucks and cars.



Wireframes



We prototyped mobile first to determine the most essential information needed on our site. For this project, we used Figma for both our wireframes and hi-fi prototype.







User Testing



We created a user testing protocol with a few different tasks and questions. We conducted user testing with 4 different Carnegie Mellon students, and our main take aways were the following:

    When asked to order a vegetarian curry for pickup,

    all users clicked on "View Menu" before "Preorder".

    The two pages are almost identical, making testers more confused on their difference.

    Testers were

    confused by the word "Preorder",

    associating it with ordering an item that hasn't been released yet.

    In our overview page of the different vendors, we created two different sections for the "Food Trucks" and "Food Cars". Testers were confused by

    what defined a "Food Car".


    In the Q&A section of the testing, multiple users mentioned that

    they would have liked a general map with the locations of all the vendors.

    There actually was one on the site, but it was in the "Locations" page of the hamburger menu, which users did not come across during their tasks.


Our team's user testing findings made it clear that changes needed to be made, so we discussed the updates we would make to the pages. My teammate added the menu, and then I took the initiative to implement the rest of the changes.


Detail Page

    Made banner more compact and informative

    Grouped payment options with order for pickup button

    Changed "Preorder" to "Order for Pickup

    Placed "Order for Pickup" above Menu so those ordering will avoid confusion of where to order.

    Added plain text menu on the bottom of the screen to make it quickly readable.


Home Page

    Replaced the landing page with a banner so users can more quickly reach their information.

    Grouped vendors by location rather than by food trucks vs food cars (which is more future-proof in case the vendors switch locations or split up into smaller groupings).

    Added campus map to the banner so users can visualize the locations of the food hubs. Our target audience, those in the CMU community, will immediately recognize the campus map without map labels.

    Made map at the top in a graphic style to make it appear as more of a decorative (yet still informative) item rather than interactive.

Reflections



This project really tought me the importance of user testing,
as our user tests helped us make significant redesigns. Though this project has mostly drawn to a close, further user testing on our iterations would surely continue to be beneficial.

I've also learned more about how to function as a group when we all have similar roles
(as opposed to being the one software engineer, the one designer, etc.). It became very clear that we were able to create better and more significant design iterations when we were able to bounce ideas off of each other, whereas working individually on a particular aspect was better for further developing and polishing a particular idea.

All in all, our group successfully worked together to create a responsive web design that would greatly benefit the Carnegie Mellon community through its ordering system and its display of information.
