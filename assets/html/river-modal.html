The Challenge

As a teamLab interactive team intern, I was tasked with redesigning the Aerial Climbing projection in their Tokyo Borderless Museum.

teamLab is an interdisciplinary art collective that explores the relationship between nature and humans through digital technology. At their Borderless Museum, the Aerial Climbing exhibit features paths of connected swing-like boards. Visitors can walk along these suspended paths, and the surrounding projection will change in response to their movements in real time.

It's not obvious that the original Aerial Climbing projection is interactive, rather than a simple pre-recorded animation.

The Aerial Climbing exhibit's original projection is of birds flying around the room and avoiding the visitors. The visitors have different colors projected onto them, so the birds will change into their respective colors when they fly nearby.

Scope & Role

I had 3 weeks to design and develop an interactive projection prototype in Unity3D from the ground up.

I worked as both the interaction designer and Unity3D developer of this project. I programmed the prototype from the ground up, with the sole exception of the flower assets (seen in the final prototype below).

Special thanks to the guidance I received from my interactive team mentors, Jasper Degens and Sakiko Misako.

Solution

Problem Analysis

What Was Working

The connection to nature, illusion of depth, and clear tracking of the visitors all worked well.

The birds connect with teamLab's greater theme of nature. The darkness of the projection helps give the illusion that the swings are suspended higher in the air. The colorful circles marking the visitor's location shows that the exhibit is following their position, making the interactivity more apparent.

What Wasn't Working

Visitors are looking at their feet and not the birds.

I first noticed that the visitors were looking down at their feet due to the instability of the swings. The birds are mostly on the walls, so the most interesting part of the projection is often missed. It therefore makes sense that the main visual feature of the projection should happen around the visitors' feet.

what makes aerial climbing unique

Most teamLab exhibitions use computer vision to track visitors position in the gallery space, so it felt important to focus on the unique qualities of this particular exhibit in my new proposal.

The exhibition has set routes that people can travel down, which is very different from the other exhibitions where people can walk around anywhere in the room.

People also generally move forward in one direction, the projection could encourage people to move forward and flow through exhibition.

ideation

Most teamLab exhibitions use computer vision to track visitors position in the gallery space, so it felt important to focus on the unique qualities of this particular exhibit in my new proposal.

I knew I wanted to emphasize the branching paths and align teamLab's greater interest in the interaction of humans and nature. I first thought about making use of the original birds, and having them fly along these paths more directly. The more acute angles in the path however could make the motion of the birds somewhat robotic looking. I then thought about flowers, but teamLab already has several exhibits with the growth of static flowers.

When thinking about nature in motion, I felt that having a river flowing through the paths would be a great fit for the exhibit.

Solution Details

the idea


My proposal for the content was to create a river on the paths where users walk.

The flow of the river increases as more people walk along the given path, and decreases overtime if no one visits it. As they walk, flowers will bloom along the sides of the river, and then disperse as they pass.

This proposal accomplishes the following:

  • The most exciting part of the projection happens where the visitors are looking.
  • The visitors can see how they affect the projection with the changing flow of the river.
  • The blooming of the flowers shows that the visitors' location is tracked in real-time.

    • Implementation

    Simulating the Exhibit

    I first had to make a simulation of people moving through the exhibit paths, since it wouldn't be practical to test with real people as I made it.

    The blue capsules represent the moving people.

    Creating the River

    A river is created along the paths people walk on. The river is visually similar to the water in other teamLab exhibitions, as seen here.

    Free-forming River

    Though the simulated people walk robotically, the prototype is programmed so that the river dynamically forms no matter where the person walks. I've also add some noise to the river flow so it's more wavy.

    "Loading” the River

    The exhibit should have a river flowing through the paths before anyone starts walking on it, so I made it possible to save the river data and then load it when you start up the exhibition. You can also see here the river flow is stronger on the paths that the visitor walks on.

    Adding Color

    Each visitor is given a color. As they walk through the exhibit, their color will flow forward in the river. This makes it more obvious to visitors how they individually affect the exhibit.
