overview

The Challenge

Professor Noah Theriault recruited us to design a mobile app his research participants could use to document their experiences of the Manila traffic crisis.

The commuting experience in Metro Manilla, Phillipines’s capital and the world’s densest city, is becoming increasingly congested and dangerous as the traffic crisis there continues. Our ultimate aim is to advance policy debates over how to handle the traffic epidemic by giving a full spectrum of middle and working class commuters the chance to have their stories heard.

Our Scope

We had 12 weeks to design an app from the ground up.

The mobile app would need to:

1.     Allow media submissions of text, audio, video, and photos.
2.     Tag submitted media with location data.
3.     Allow researchers to sort data through various filters.
4.     Cater to a diverse pool of recruited Manila residents.
5.     Be scalable as a publically released app.

Our Solution

Saan Ka (“Where Are You?”)

A human-centered data collection app aimed at helping researchers record and amplify the voices of under-recognized populations.

Additional features not listed in the overview include onboarding, a history of past submissions available for edit, an inbox for messages from researchers, and an account page. The complete collection of screens created can be seen in our prototype video at the bottom of the case study.

Mapping Feelings

Pins on the map show users where they’ve documented and their primary feeling at that location. Users can toggle between seeing just their markers or all public ones. Public pins will be anonymized.

The current emojis on the map are placeholders from FB messenger— they will be replaced with custom ones in the real app.

Documenting Experiences

The documenting experience is streamlined with an optional checklist format to balance the needs of both researchers and participants.

Filtering Data

Both researchers and participants are able to filter the pins on the map to see patterns in the data (eg. finding a correlation between frustration and rainy days, rush hour, etc.).

next steps

App Development & Scaling

Our hi-fidelity design has been passed to developers— after the app is complete, smart phones with Saan Ka will be distributed to participants for the duration of the study. We’ve designed the app in hopes that it may grow beyond the participants of the study and be used amongst other residents of Metro Manila.

We suggested to Professor Theriault that additional user testing should be conducted with Manila residents, and a desktop version would ideally be created for researchers. Custom feeling emojis will also be created to replace our placeholders for the real app.

    our process

    Our design process included paper prototyping, user flow mapping, lo- to hi-fidelity prototyping, user testing, and several iterations.

    Here are some highlights from the process, as well as design decisions that came from it.

    Paper prototyping

    We each created paper prototypes to find an app flow that most easily allowed on-the-go users to submit their reports.

    We then discussed the pros and cons of each, and ultimately combined the best aspects of each.

    User flow mapping

    When working out the user flow, we decided to have the Manila map be the home screen so users can quickly mark the location of their report.

    The location data of the submitted images is one of the most important pieces of data for researchers. The map gives a visual for users adding this data.

    user testing

    While creating lo-, mid-, and hi-fidelity prototypes, we user tested with our client, did think-alouds for usability with 8 non-affiliated locals, and did internal user testing while commuting.

    This allowed us to correct some assumption we made along the way. We didn’t have access to Manila residents, and therefore conducted user testing in Pittsburgh.

    Halving the Onboarding

    During user testing we realized the onboarding was far too long.

    We originally had users input their commute information so some information could auto-fill when submissions were being made. We realized that this took too much time in onboarding, and that it may also feel somewhat intrusive in terms of required location tracking.

    home screen iterations

    We changed the language and user flow the home page for more intuitive report submissions.

    reflection

    Working with a Client, Designing with a Team, and Experiencing “We are not the user"

    This was a great experience designing within a team of designers. I saw how we could generate ideas together, and later on divide work to create a seamless exquisite corpse. When working with a client, I learned how we need to really plan ahead in order to coordinate times to meet and collaborate.

    Finally, this was a great example of how there’s times when we could have made dangerous assumptions of our users. Though we did user research and talked to Professor Theriault to correct our assumptions, in hindsight I would have pushed more to user test with Manila residents.
